# The Skill Ecosystem

My laptop has twenty-six skills installed. They're files, mostly — `SKILL.md` each, a few with a `references/` subfolder for the corpus material that didn't belong in the rulebook. From the outside they look like a pile of utilities. From the inside they're a working answer to a question I've been asking for a year.

The question is whether the thing I built at Oracle works on one person.

The thing I built from the research I did at Oracle is Domain Foundation — a methodology for encoding institutional design expertise into a form a model can reach at generation time. Four layers of knowledge, each owned by the role that produces it. The methodology's whole bet is that specific institutional context is what separates commodity AI output from the output that reflects a specific organization's judgment. I wrote about it at length [elsewhere](/work/domain-foundation/). What I haven't written about is what happened when I started running the methodology on the only design org I'm still inside — mine.

Here's what that looks like.

## One rule, not a hundred

The voice skill is called `jeremy-voice`. It's twenty pages of rules for writing in my voice, with a meta-rule at the top: *when the prose feels off, it's almost always because it shifted from reporting to thesizing. Return it to reporting and the voice comes back.* Everything else in the skill is an instance of that single rule. Anti-patterns catalog. Per-medium extensions for case studies and blog posts. Calibration links to live posts.

The twenty pages aren't a recipe. The meta-rule is. The rest is the corpus that lets a model tell whether a draft is drifting.

This is the shape Domain Foundation takes when a human-sized institution is writing it: one load-bearing rule and a corpus of examples sharp enough to apply it.

## Scale it down and it still works

The skill I'm most fond of is called `kc-topography-analyst`. It's five pages of Kansas City terrain analysis scoped to Meshtastic mesh networking — where to put a LoRa node, why the Missouri River bluffs matter, what happens to a 915 MHz signal crossing the glaciated plains north of the river.

It's absurdly specific. "Penn Valley Park / Hospital Hill area: downtown-adjacent, elevated, overlooking both rivers." You will not find that in any general radio resource. It's the intersection of my city and my hobby, written down with enough precision that a model can reason from it.

The original claim of Domain Foundation was that encoded institutional expertise would let design orgs do work that didn't look like everyone else's. The same thing turns out to be true at individual-practitioner scale. A generic radio-propagation answer is the average. A KC-specific one reflects ten years of living here and a weekend spent mapping elevation by hand. The corpus does the differentiating.

## Creative practice, same shape

`songwriting` covers my Red Dirt Americana practice. The meta-rule: *the song already exists somewhere — in fragments, phrases, residue. The job is to excavate, not generate.* Which is the creative-practice version of "reporting, not thesizing." Same move. Different medium.

There's a fictional Workshop in the Crossroads district of Kansas City that every Cocktail Napkin thumbnail is set in — same room each time, different state. Its skill is called `jeremys-workshop`. Full floor plan, zone inventory, lighting rules, a spec file that's the source of truth so no generated image drifts off-model. Domain Foundation applied to a year's worth of blog thumbnails.

A lot of the skills are like this. The mechanism stays the same across domains. What changes is the corpus material it's acting on.

## The system itself

`omnifocus-expert` isn't craft knowledge. It's my task system, encoded — the eight projects I actually have, the seven tags I actually use, the priority rules for how my job search gets sequenced, the rule that flagged means "committed for today" not "wish." A generic task-management skill would be worthless. This one works because it knows what system it's governing.

`home-assistant-expert` is the same idea applied to my 2013 Mac Pro running Home Assistant in a Docker container (not Supervised, which changes everything about which integrations work). `obsidian-pkm` describes the four zones my vault is actually organized into. A research-vocabulary skill carries the terms from the pre-Domain Foundation project — validation stack, role inversion, the velocity paradox — into any new conversation that touches that thinking.

Operational knowledge encodes the same way craft knowledge does. The trick is naming what your system actually is, not what a textbook says a system should be.

## The gap

I'm not going to pretend this is complete. I don't have a skill for photography. I don't have one for printmaking, even though that zone is in the Workshop spec. I have a good one for the Ghost theme this site runs on — [it's new](https://github.com/jeremyfuksa/ghost) — but I didn't have it a month ago.

The corpus reflects what I've worked on heavily enough to write the encoding for. What I haven't is still in my head, where it does nobody's model any good. A practitioner-level Domain Foundation isn't a finished thing. It's an ongoing encoding problem, bounded by what you can bring yourself to externalize.

## What this proves

Domain Foundation is a bet about organizations. The bet is that encoded institutional expertise would let design teams do work that a PM with a good prompt couldn't reproduce. The research that led there happened inside Oracle Health, at the scale of a design org with its own design system team, human factors researchers, and a PhD on staff whose job included tracking healthcare regulatory and safety practice.

The practitioner version is smaller. It's one person with twenty-six skill files and a gap list he's honest about. But it's the same mechanism, and it works. The difference between a model answering as an averaging machine and a model answering with a reflection of specific practice turns out to be the corpus, regardless of whether the corpus is a healthcare org's human factors research or a songwriter's notes on Wormy Dog Saloon.

That's what I can say from a year of running this on myself. Orgs are a harder encoding problem than individuals, for reasons that mostly reduce to politics. But the individual-scale case is settled. The methodology scales down.
