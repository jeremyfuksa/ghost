# jeremyOS

The [Moonbird research](/work/moonbird/) was hitting peak momentum when the layoff happened. The work was generating its own evidence — small proofs starting to stack. It was the easiest thing in the world to keep going. I took what I'd learned and pointed it at the only institution still in front of me. Myself.

If you've been reading my other pieces, [Domain Foundation](/work/domain-foundation/) is the basis for all of this. The institutional knowledge I'm encoding at this scale is mine — the experience I carry, the anecdotes I keep returning to, the instincts I've built over thirty years of design practice. I'm tying all of it together with one sole purpose: finding a new job.

## The Setup

The OS runs on a handful of pieces. Commitment lives in OmniFocus — eight projects, seven tags, a skill called `omnifocus-expert` that routes anything I capture mid-conversation to the right place. Thinking lives in Obsidian across four zones, governed by `obsidian-pkm` and `obsidian-cli`. Procedural knowledge lives in the skills themselves — twenty-six of them, the ecosystem I [wrote about separately](/work/skill-ecosystem/). Automation lives in a self-hosted n8n instance that lets the subsystems talk to each other without me carrying messages between them. And a 2013 Mac Pro in the basement runs Home Assistant, extending the OS into the physical world because the physical world is where the work actually happens.

That's the tour. The interesting part is what running it through the job search proved.

## What It Proved

The thesis predicts that encoded expertise compounds. Every decision written down is one fewer decision to make next time, which frees the session for new work. At enterprise scale I'd argued this to design leaders and wait-listed the proof. On myself, I could watch it.

Six months in, the pattern held. The site you're reading got rebuilt against its own skills. The job search runs on captured commitments and routed tasks. Writing goes through `jeremy-voice`. Thumbnails go through `jeremys-workshop`. A network of subsystems that didn't exist a year ago now holds more context than I can, and the sessions I spend with it are about the next thing instead of reconstructing what I already knew last week.

Which is the whole claim. At any scale that has recurring decisions — enterprise design org or one person running a job search — the methodology does the same thing. It keeps context warm across sessions so the foreground work can be about something new.

## Where It Breaks

Plenty of places. OmniFocus and Obsidian don't share a URL scheme, so linking a task to a note still means pasting a path and hoping it doesn't rot. The n8n instance has ten workflows I've sketched and three that actually run. The Home Assistant voice pipeline works for simple things and crashes on anything complex. The photography zone of the Workshop doesn't have its own skill yet.

Some of those seams are because the encoding is hard. Some are because I haven't done it yet. Either way, the gap list itself is its own artifact — I know what to work on because I wrote down what's missing.

## The Part That Matters

I don't think most people should build what I've built. Twenty-six skills is a lot. Four Obsidian zones is a lot. A self-hosted automation layer is a lot. The specific configuration is mine because the specific practice is mine.

What travels is the proof. The layoff ended the enterprise version of this work before it could finish. The job search is where I got to finish it at a scale small enough to hold. The thesis works.
