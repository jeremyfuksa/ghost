## The frame

"Easy There, Oppenheimer" as the title is doing the whole job. Skills are a breakthrough that works unreasonably well, and the mechanism that makes them work is inseparable from their destructive potential. Colette is the atomic bomb: a genuine innovation that solves a real problem nobody else has solved, whose logic carries its own shadow. The title isn't a wink — it's the structural frame.

## Raw material pulled from the primer draft

### The Colette-as-atomic-bomb framing

The mechanism that preserves Colette's judgment is the same mechanism that argues she isn't needed anymore. That isn't a caveat. That's the move itself.

- What makes her indispensable is exactly the thing a skill can capture
- Once it's captured, the argument for why the role still belongs to a person gets weaker by the hour
- Not a future risk — a structural feature of the mechanism

### The Meta news as the industrial-scale demonstration

> Yesterday Meta announced it's installing software on US employees' computers to capture their keystrokes and screen activity to train AI agents — explicitly to build "digital twins of human problem-solving patterns."
>
> Source: [TechCrunch, April 21 2026](https://www.techcrunch.com/2026/04/21/meta-will-record-employees-keystrokes-and-use-it-to-train-its-ai-models/)

This is the Colette mechanism at industrial scale. A $1T company operating on the premise that the tacit knowledge of expert practitioners is worth extracting by surveillance, at scale, for training purposes.

### The "enigma to commonplace" transformation

> You're asking someone to sit with you and narrate what makes them good at their job. What comes out of that conversation goes into a file your whole team will draw on — which means the conversation itself is the moment she stops being irreplaceable. In a heartbeat, the thing that made her an enigma is the thing making her dispensable.

The line is probably the strongest sentence from tonight's drafting. Save it.

### The open honesty that had to come out

> I don't have an answer for that. I'm not sure the answer exists yet.

This is the register the piece should live in. Not editorialized, not hand-wringing, not optimistic — just the honest acknowledgment that a genuine breakthrough is also a genuine problem and that the ethics haven't been worked out.

## Threads worth developing

1. **Tacit knowledge ingestion is the real research frontier.** See `project_tacit_knowledge_thesis.md` in memory. The architecture of skills/corpora is solved. The ingestion of tacit knowledge from practitioners is not, and it's where most of the actual difficulty lives. That thread belongs here.

2. **The "best practice" trap.** Every vendor pitch frames this as good hygiene — capture tribal knowledge, reduce bus factor, enable AI at scale. What that framing dodges is the labor-side question: you're being asked to participate in the commodification of your own expertise. The right framing names this.

3. **Oppenheimer's actual biography is load-bearing.** He didn't regret the physics. He regretted what the physics enabled. The piece's frame should hold that distinction — the mechanism isn't wrong, the use isn't inevitable, and the people who build the mechanism have a responsibility to name what's possible with it.

4. **The responsibility of the person doing the encoding.** The skill author is the person operating the lens. What they choose to capture, what they choose to leave out, what calibration they apply — those are editorial decisions that shape what the mechanism becomes. That's its own essay.
